Magnus Carlsen has been the world's top-rated chess player since 2011, and he held the world championship from 2013 until he walked away from the title in 2023. By almost any measure he is the strongest player of his generation, and arguably of any generation. So there is something disorienting about seeing his name at the very bottom of the standings at Norway Chess 2026, the elite super-tournament staged each year in his home country.

The turn came in Round 5, when he lost to American grandmaster Wesley So. It dropped the 35-year-old to last place in a six-player field he had been favoured to win when the event opened a week earlier. For a player who has spent more than a decade as the man everyone else measures themselves against, a last-place marker beside his name reads almost like a typo.

It isn't one, and that is the interesting part. The gap between Carlsen and the rest of the world's best has been narrowing for years. A generation of players raised on engines and online blitz has closed the distance, and on any given day the field is now deep enough that even the favourite can be ground down. A bad round no longer corrects itself automatically the way it once seemed to.

None of which means he is finished here. Five rounds remain, and Carlsen has climbed out of holes far deeper than this one, often by simply refusing to lose a second game. If anyone in this field can turn a last-place start into a tournament victory, it is the man whose whole career has been built on relentless, patient pressure.

But the image lingers regardless of how the standings finish: the best player in the world, stumbling in his own backyard, in front of a home crowd that came to watch him win. It is a useful reminder that dominance in chess is never permanent, and that the distance between first and last at the very top has quietly become very small.

Five rounds remain. The Daily Gambit will be watching.
