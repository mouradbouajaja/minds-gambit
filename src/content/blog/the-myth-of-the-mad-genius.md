## The Myth of the Mad Genius

*“The connection between genius and insanity has been long and widely recognized.” — Cesare Lombroso, 1891.*

*He was wrong. This article is about why chess believed him anyway.*

### The story chess tells about itself

There is a story the chess world has told about itself for a century and a half, and it goes like this: chess drives people mad. The game is so deep, so consuming, that the minds drawn to it get pushed toward the edge — and the greatest minds get pushed the furthest, because genius brings you closest to the abyss.

It is a powerful story. It is also a myth. Not a myth in the sense that nobody ever suffered — the suffering was real, and we will look at it honestly. A myth in the older sense: a story a community tells to give itself meaning. And this particular story was never built from evidence. It was built from a handful of dramatic cases, retold and reshaped by a culture that wanted to believe its game was more than a game.

Here is why that matters: the myth does not just describe suffering. It manufactures it — and then it excuses it.

### A young man from New Orleans

Paul Morphy learned chess as a boy watching his father and uncle play. By twelve he was beating the strongest players in New Orleans. By twenty he was the strongest player in the world. His tour of Europe in 1858 was one of the most dominant performances in the history of competition — he gave the best players alive a head start and beat them anyway. They compared him to Mozart.

And then, at twenty-two, he stopped. He went home to become a lawyer, the career he had trained for. The world would not let him. Wherever he went, he was the chess player — the celebrity of a game he no longer wished to play. The respectable professional life he wanted was closed to him by his own fame.

Decades later — decades — he grew reclusive. He developed paranoid fears. He died in 1884, at forty-seven.

Look carefully at the order of events, because the myth depends on you not looking. Morphy’s troubles began *after* he left chess, not while he played it. His world had been wrecked by the Civil War. His profession was blocked. His identity was stolen by celebrity. That is a tragedy of identity, not a tale about a board game devouring a mind. But “chess destroyed its greatest genius” made a better story — so the timeline was quietly compressed, the war forgotten, and Morphy became the myth’s founding martyr.

### The first champion, the second martyr

Wilhelm Steinitz, the first official World Champion, did more than anyone to turn chess from romantic art into a science that could be studied and taught. For this, the world paid him almost nothing. He lived most of his life in poverty, feuding publicly, resented and resenting. At fifty-eight he lost the title that had been his entire identity. In 1900, in his mid-sixties, he was committed to an institution, and he died that year in a pauper’s grave.

An aging man. Decades of poverty. The loss of the one thing that defined him. Possibly physical illness on top. None of this requires chess as an explanation — but chess is the explanation the chess world chose, because chess is the explanation the myth demanded. The pattern was set, and every later case was fed into it: Rubinstein’s social anxiety became “chess madness.” Alekhine’s alcoholism became “genius consumed by its own fire.” Always the game. Never the poverty, the isolation, or the total absence of anyone whose job it was to help.

### Why the myth survives

Myths live as long as they are useful, and this one earns its keep three times over.

It flatters the game: a pastime that can destroy a mind like Morphy’s must be profound — not a hobby but a dangerous reach for intellectual fire. It flatters the players: the tortured genius is a far better self-image than the truth of most chess lives, which is long quiet study, modest prizes, and the daily management of rating anxiety. And — this is the part that costs lives — it normalizes suffering. If madness simply comes with the territory, then your anxiety, your obsession, your black mood after a loss are not problems. They are proof of commitment. A badge.

A culture that hands out suffering as a badge does not build support systems. Why would it? Nothing is wrong. It is just chess being chess.

### The science that was not science

The myth borrowed its respectability from a fashionable nineteenth-century theory. Cesare Lombroso argued that genius and insanity were two faces of the same biological flaw. The idea swept Europe, and it fit chess like a glove: the brighter the player, the nearer the madness.

Modern psychology has dismantled Lombroso. What actually holds up is almost the reverse — and it is more unsettling. Stories themselves do damage. Tell a community that its passion leads to madness, and that expectation becomes a weight its members carry; tell a struggling player that struggle is his destiny, and he will not ask for help. The mechanism was never biology. It was always the story.

### The machine that keeps it alive

Open the chess shelf and count: the biographies are rise, triumph, decline, fall — Morphy, Steinitz, Alekhine, Fischer. The champions who lived steady, healthy lives — Karpov, Anand, many more — get fewer books, because stability is not a story the myth knows how to tell. Listen to commentary: players “collapse,” “lose their nerve,” “go to pieces” — as if every tournament were a psychiatric event. Watch the films: the chess player on screen is the brilliant misfit at the edge of pathology, from Zweig’s *The Royal Game* — a work of fiction that the culture reads like a documentary — to *The Queen’s Gambit*, which brought millions to the game while teaching them, gently and beautifully, that brilliance and breakdown are a package deal.

There are other stories — *Brooklyn Castle*, *Queen of Katwe* — stories about what chess builds rather than what it destroys. Notice how few they are.

### Who pays the bill

Not Morphy. Not Steinitz. The bill is paid today, by ordinary players at every level who absorbed the myth without ever reading it.

Sociologists call it a silencing effect. The player who lies awake replaying a lost game says nothing — everyone replays games. The player drowning in self-loathing after a defeat seeks no help — everyone feels bad after a loss. The player who senses the game has become a compulsion calls it dedication — because intensity is what the culture applauds. The suffering is treated as the entry fee, so no one asks for a refund.

And notice who the myth is even about. Morphy, Steinitz, Fischer — it is a story of male genius and male suffering. The pressures carried by women in chess do not fit its template, so the culture simply does not see them. I wrote about that invisible weight in [The Queen’s Burden](/blog/the-queens-burden).

Here is the truth the myth was built to hide: it is not the sixty-four squares that cause suffering. It is the rating system that quantifies human worth, the tournament culture that rewards endurance over wellbeing, the absence of psychological support at every level — and the stories a community tells itself about what it means to play. Chess can build patience, resilience, and belonging — the school programs prove it daily — but only when the game is wrapped in structures of care. The same game, without the care, can quietly take a person apart.

### A better question

For a hundred and fifty years the chess world has asked: *what is it about chess that drives people mad?* The question has produced nothing but mythology — and it erased everyone who did not fit. Theophilus Thompson, born enslaved, published a book of chess problems in 1874 — one of the first Black American chess authors, a man for whom chess was ambition asserted against impossible barriers. The myth has no shelf for him. It never built one.

There is a better question: *what is it about the culture of chess that fails to protect the people who play it?* The first question is a shrug dressed up as a tragedy. The second one has answers — and the answers can be acted on.

A game measures what you did at the board. It must never be allowed to measure what you are worth. The myth of the mad genius is, at bottom, the failure to keep those two things apart.

And no life shows the price of that failure more clearly than the boy from Brooklyn who became the most famous chess player in history — celebrated as a genius by a whole civilization that never once stopped to ask whether the genius was okay. Bobby Fischer’s story is next.

### Questions for you, the reader

Take one of these to your club, your classroom, or the dinner table:

1. Where did you first meet the “mad chess genius” — a film, a novel, a club legend? Did you believe it at the time?
2. Morphy’s troubles began after he left the game. Why do you think the chess world still tells the story the other way around?
3. A story can flatter a community and harm it at the same time. What story like that does *your* world — your job, your sport, your scene — tell about itself?
4. Commentators say a player “collapsed” or “went to pieces.” How does that language change what young players believe is happening inside them?
5. If suffering is worn as a badge of commitment, who learns to stay silent? Have you ever been the one staying silent?
6. Name one concrete thing a chess club, school, or federation could do tomorrow to protect the people who play. What is stopping them?
