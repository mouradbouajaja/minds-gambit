## The Queen’s Burden

*“It’s not the chess that’s exhausting. It’s everything around the chess.” — a woman grandmaster, interviewed for this book*

### She is nineteen, and she is thinking about quitting

She is rated well above 2200. She has played for her country at three Olympiads and won her national women’s championship twice. By any honest measure she is one of the strongest young players her federation has. And she is thinking about walking away.

Not because she stopped loving the game. Not because her talent ran out. She is thinking about quitting because of everything that came *with* the game, year after year, since she was seven: the comments about her appearance, the opponents who would not shake her hand, the coaches who told her she was “pretty good for a girl,” the online abuse that follows her every time she streams, and the quiet rule that says a strong result from a woman is a fluke while a weak one is proof.

She is a real person. And here is the uncomfortable part: her story is not unusual. Ask the women in competitive chess and most will shrug — this is simply the weather. But weather you stand in for twelve years, without shelter, takes a toll.

### The numbers, and the comfortable story

Women are roughly fifteen percent of rated chess players in the world. Above 2500 — the level of elite international play — they are closer to one percent. In all of history, exactly one woman, Judit Polgár, has reached the world’s top ten.

The chess world has long had a comfortable explanation for this: men are simply better at chess. It is a convenient story, because it asks nothing of anyone. If the gap is biology, the culture is innocent and nothing needs to change.

The science does not back the comfortable story.

### The invisible weight

Psychologists call it *stereotype threat*, and the idea is simple enough for a ten-year-old to understand. When you are told, in a hundred small ways, that people like you are not good at something — and then you sit down to do exactly that thing — part of your mind spends the whole game answering that voice instead of playing. Chess demands every drop of your attention. Stereotype threat quietly steals a share of it.

The research bears this out on the board. Studies of thousands of real games found that women underperform their own level most precisely when playing male opponents — in the situations where their gender is made to matter most. Read that again: the gap is not simply a gap in ability. Part of it is *manufactured by the room*.

So every woman at the board is playing two games at once. One against the opponent. One against the story that says she should not be there. The second game does not show up in the score sheet. It shows up in the exhaustion.

### The weather

The hostility women describe is rarely dramatic. It is ambient — a background hum. “You don’t look like a chess player.” Unsolicited advice from weaker players. “She’s good *for a woman*.” The staring. The chat rooms during a stream, where any display of skill is met with “she must be getting help.”

And when a woman names any of this, a second hum starts: she is overreacting, she cannot take a joke, she is making excuses. So most stop mentioning it. Enduring quietly becomes part of the job.

Here is what that costs. Girls enter chess in nearly the same numbers as boys — school programs see it every year, equal enthusiasm, equal fire. Then adolescence arrives, and the girls begin to leave. By the time you reach the serious tournament hall, they are nearly gone. The chess world calls this natural selection. It is nothing of the kind. The pipeline is not the problem. The destination is the problem.

### The proof and the price

Judit Polgár answered the biology story for good. Grandmaster at fifteen, breaking Bobby Fischer’s record. Peak rating among the top ten players alive. Victories over eleven world champions — including Garry Kasparov, who had once dismissed women’s chess and revised his opinion only after she beat him.

But look at what the proof required. Polgár was raised by a father who made her chess education his life’s project, who shielded his daughters from the worst of the culture and trained them under conditions no ordinary family could ever recreate. Her career does not prove the system works for women. It proves what it takes to succeed *despite* the system — and that price is one no one should have to pay. Even she carried an extra burden every time she sat down: every game a referendum, every loss an argument handed to people who were waiting for it.

### The paradox of the protected room

Chess maintains a parallel world for women — separate titles, separate tournaments, separate rankings. It was built with good intentions: a protected space, achievable milestones, visible encouragement.

But the protected room whispers something too. The Woman Grandmaster title sits about two hundred rating points below the open one, and everyone in the hall knows it. A separate ladder, set lower, tells every woman exactly what the institution expects of her — and expectations like that have a way of being absorbed.

The answer is probably not to demolish the room. It is to change what the room is *for*: from shelter into springboard — women’s events built as stepping stones into open competition, with coaching, mentorship, and a clear path through the door, not a comfortable corner to be kept in.

### The Queen’s Gambit test

In 2020, a television series did what a century of federation programs never managed: it sent women and girls into chess in record numbers. Beth Harmon — brilliant, flawed, competing in the open section and winning — gave the world an image of a woman in chess that chess itself had never created.

They came by the millions. And they walked into the same tournament halls, the same coaching structures, the same chat rooms. The culture had not changed in anticipation of their arrival. Within a year or two, many were gone.

It was, without anyone planning it, a perfect experiment. It proved the interest was always there. What is missing is not women who want to play. It is a chess world capable of keeping them.

### What change would actually look like

None of this is mysterious, and little of it is expensive. Count properly: collect real data on who enters, who leaves, and when — today, astonishingly, no one does. Train every coach in safeguarding and in the simple psychology of expectation, because a coach who understands the invisible weight can help lift it. Equalize the prize funds. Put women in the rooms where the decisions are made. And tell different stories — so the ten-year-old girl walking into her first club sees women on the posters not as a footnote or an exception, but as part of the game’s story. Because they always were.

### The strongest piece

The queen is the most powerful piece on the board. She moves in any direction, across any distance. The game cannot be played without her.

She is named for a role that the world of chess has diminished, doubted, and asked to be grateful. The women who play feel that irony every day — the gap between the queen’s power on the board and their own standing beside it.

The signal under all the noise is clear: this gap is not nature. It is culture. And culture — unlike biology — can be changed by people who decide to change it.

### Questions for you, the reader

Take one of these to dinner tonight, or to your club, or to the comments of our next stream:

1. Have you ever carried an invisible weight into a competition — or watched someone else carry one? What did it cost?
2. If girls enter chess with the same fire as boys and leave in their teens, what do you think they are meeting along the way?
3. Do separate women’s titles protect women, or quietly tell them what is expected of them? Could both be true at once?
4. It took Judit Polgár beating the world number one to change one famous mind. What would it take to change yours — or your club’s?
5. If you ran a chess club, what is the one thing you would change tomorrow so a ten-year-old girl never stops loving the game?
6. The queen is the strongest piece on the board. What would chess look like the day the game treats its women that way?
