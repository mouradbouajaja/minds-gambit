When Wesley So sat down to face Magnus Carlsen on Wednesday in Oslo, the smart money was on the Norwegian. Carlsen, 35, is the world's top-rated chess player at 2840 — a number he has held essentially without interruption for fifteen years. So, 32 and Filipino-American, is two hundred rating points below, and the quietest of the three U.S. grandmasters currently in the world's top ten. The smart money lost.

So defeated Carlsen in their classical game in Round 5 of the 14th Norway Chess super-tournament, taking second place in the standings and dropping the world number one to last. World Champion Gukesh Dommaraju of India scored his first classical victory of the event with a win over compatriot Praggnanandhaa Rameshbabu, landing in third. Five rounds remain at Deichman Bjorvika in Oslo before the June 5 finish.

A single classical game is a single classical game. The picture around it is bigger. Three Americans — Nakamura, the world #2 at 2792 and one of the most-watched chess streamers on the internet; Caruana, the world #3 at 2788 who lost a 12-game match for the world title in 2018 by a tiebreak that still haunts him; and So, the quiet third — now sit in the world's top ten. Nakamura and Caruana enter the FIDE Candidates Tournament as its top two seeds. American GM Sam Sevian, 25 and rated 2680, has begun showing up in elite tournament fields too.

For most readers of this paper, the last American world champion is a name from before they were born. Bobby Fischer beat Soviet World Champion Boris Spassky in Reykjavík, Iceland, in 1972 — a Cold War contest as much as a chess match — and then, almost immediately, disappeared into exile and silence. The fifty-three years since have produced strong American players, dedicated coaches, scholastic programs in nearly every city. The top table of world chess has been elsewhere: Moscow, then Beijing, then Chennai.

That is no longer true.

Whether or not an American takes Gukesh's crown this cycle, the structural reality is changed. The U.S. Chess Federation has more elite-rated players than at any point since Fischer's reign. The Saint Louis Chess Club — the Missouri institution founded in 2008 by billionaire Rex Sinquefield — runs the U.S. Championship and the American Cup every year with a budget that rivals any European event. Young Americans like Sevian are competing in fields that, a decade ago, would have been closed to anyone outside the European-Soviet pipeline.

The Daily Gambit will cover this cycle closely — not only the games, but the structures: the academies that build these players, the federations that fight to keep them, and the cities, including Pittsburgh, that produce them. American chess has stopped chasing the world. It has joined it.
